Patient journey

From admission to stable community follow-up

Each stage has a clear clinical purpose, a responsible staff member, and documentation that follows the patient throughout the full episode of care.

Step 1

Admission and registration

Confirm identity, referral reason, guardian or next-of-kin contacts, consent, immediate risks, belongings, and bed assignment.

  • Open patient file and assign unique clinic number.
  • Screen for suicide, aggression, withdrawal, intoxication, and medical emergency.
  • Record current medication and known allergies before the first dose.
Step 2

Clinical and psychosocial assessment

Complete psychiatric history, mental status exam, physical screening, substance-use history, family context, and spiritual needs.

  • Set diagnosis, care level, observation level, and initial treatment plan.
  • Document patient strengths, risks, triggers, and preferred calming strategies.
  • Agree on a practical recovery plan with patient and family where appropriate.
Step 3

Treatment during the residential stay

Provide medication, daily nursing observation, psychosocial sessions, spiritual care, recreation, hygiene support, and family updates.

  • Record medication administration, missed doses, response, and side effects.
  • Review risk level and treatment progress during ward rounds.
  • Prepare life skills, routine, and relapse prevention before discharge planning.
Step 4

Discharge readiness

Discharge when the patient is clinically stable, has a safe destination, understands medication, and has a follow-up plan.

  • Prepare discharge summary, medication supply, warning signs, and appointment date.
  • Hold family or caregiver handover meeting.
  • Document transport plan, community support, and crisis contact route.
Step 5

Follow-up and re-entry support

Continue care with phone calls, clinic reviews, medication checks, relapse prevention, and early re-admission when needed.

  • Call within 48 hours after discharge where possible.
  • Review medication adherence, symptoms, sleep, substance use, and family stress.
  • Escalate urgently if the patient becomes unsafe or stops essential treatment.

Admission desk

Admit each patient with a complete starting record

Admission is the first clinical safety checkpoint. The goal is to understand the patient, reduce immediate risk, protect rights, and begin treatment without losing important information.

Admission criteria

  • Male patient requiring medium-level residential psychiatric care.
  • Needs safe shelter, treatment supervision, or structured recovery support.
  • Medically stable enough for clinic admission, or referred after hospital stabilization.
  • Consent and guardian involvement documented according to local law and policy.

Initial documents

  • Patient identification and clinic number.
  • Referral note or family admission request.
  • Medication list, allergy record, and medical history.
  • Risk assessment, consent forms, belongings inventory, and bed assignment.

During stay

Medication, daily care, and recovery routines

Residential psychiatric care works best when medication and support services are recorded in the same care plan, reviewed regularly, and explained in a way the patient and family understand.

Medication management

  1. Prescriber confirms diagnosis, allergies, current medicines, and target symptoms.
  2. Nursing staff record every administered dose, refusal, missed dose, and observed effect.
  3. Side effects, sleep, appetite, movement symptoms, and physical observations are monitored.
  4. Medication is reviewed during ward rounds and before discharge supply is issued.

Psychosocial support

  1. Individual counselling and psychoeducation begin after stabilization.
  2. Groups cover relapse prevention, substance-use recovery, communication, and routine building.
  3. Family sessions support understanding, boundaries, medication adherence, and safe return home.
  4. Meaningful activities help restore confidence, self-care, and social functioning.

Daily observation levels

Routine: stable patient with normal ward checks.
Close: increased checks for risk, confusion, withdrawal, or severe symptoms.
High: constant or near-constant observation and urgent clinical review.

Discharge planning

Leave with a plan, medicine, and a named follow-up route

Discharge begins early in the stay. The team should confirm clinical stability, safe accommodation, caregiver understanding, medication continuity, and follow-up before the patient leaves.

Discharge checklist

  • Clinical review completed
  • Risk level reduced or managed
  • Medication supply prepared
  • Side effects explained
  • Family handover completed
  • Relapse signs documented
  • Follow-up date confirmed
  • Transport and destination confirmed

Aftercare

Follow-up keeps recovery connected after discharge

Follow-up should check symptoms, medicine adherence, family stress, relapse signs, sleep, substance use, and social reintegration. High-risk patients need faster contact and easier return for review.

48 hours

First call

Confirm safe arrival, medicine use, sleep, immediate risks, and family questions.

1 week

Clinic review

Review symptoms, side effects, adherence, substance use, and appointment barriers.

1 month

Recovery plan

Adjust medicines where needed, strengthen routine, and update family support plan.

3 to 6 months

Longer support

Track relapse prevention, functioning, work or study goals, and community participation.
